## Findings / caveats

- **This w4a4-kv8 step500 is the strongest quantized model on BOTH metrics.** Its GPQA (0.5808) *ties bf16* and beats every other quantized variant, and its RULER 13-task mean (0.837) is the highest of any model here. Notable given it's the most aggressive quantization (activations also 4-bit). The QAD training on this checkpoint recovered the w4a4 accuracy gap completely on these two benchmarks — cf. the naive w4a4 NVFP4 in 001 (GPQA 0.505 / RULER 0.775).
- **`niah_multikey_2` = 0.980** here vs 0.18–0.80 for all other models — this single subtask is the entire reason the RULER mean is high (see the inversion analysis below). It is a behavioral/completeness effect, not evidence that w4a4 quantization improves long-context retrieval.
- **`ruler_vt` = 0.000** again (every model, all runs) — systematic non-thinking task/format artifact, not a quant effect. Kept in the 13-task mean.
- All 14 jobs reached intended totals with 0 errored trials.

## Why does w4a4-kv8 RULER (0.837) beat bf16 (0.795)?

The result looks absurd — the most aggressively quantized model beats full-precision on long-context RULER. It is **not** "quantization made the model better." Three points, in order of importance:

**1. The entire gap is one subtask (`niah_multikey_2`); everything else ties or favors bf16.**
Per-task RULER, bf16 vs w4a4-kv8-step500:

| task | bf16 | w4a4-kv8 | Δ |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| single_1/2/3, multikey_1, multivalue, multiquery | 1.000 | 1.000 | 0 |
| **niah_multikey_2** | **0.360** | **0.980** | **+0.620** |
| niah_multikey_3 | 0.980 | 1.000 | +0.020 |
| ruler_cwe | 0.878 | 0.802 | −0.076 |
| ruler_fwe | 0.867 | 0.880 | +0.013 |
| ruler_vt | 0.000 | 0.000 | 0 |
| ruler_qa_hotpot | 0.640 | 0.580 | −0.060 |
| ruler_qa_squad | 0.608 | 0.642 | +0.034 |
| **mean (13)** | **0.795** | **0.837** | **+0.042** |

`niah_multikey_2` alone contributes +0.620/13 = **+0.048** to the mean; the other 12 tasks net **−0.005**. Remove multikey_2 and bf16 wins. On the genuinely hard tasks (cwe, qa_hotpot) bf16 is still ahead.

**2. These are different models, not "the same model quantized."**
The 001 bf16 is the base Qwen3-8B. The w4a4-kv8-step500 is a QAD (quantization-aware distillation) *student*, further trained for 500 steps. Any capability/behavior difference is attributable to that extra training, not to quantization. (Naive w4a4 without QAD — the 001 NVFP4 — scores RULER 0.775, *below* bf16, as expected.)

**3. The mechanism on multikey_2 is answer completeness, confirmed by response length.**
`niah_multikey_2` requires returning **multiple** retrieved values; RULER scores by string match. Raw `output_tokens` on that job:

| model | multikey_2 mean out-tokens | p50 | score |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| bf16 | 10.9 | 11 | 0.360 |
| w4a4-kv8-step500 | 24.4 | 24 | 0.980 |

The base bf16 **under-generates** — it emits ~11 tokens (roughly one value) and stops, so it misses most of the required needles and scores 0.36. The QAD student learned to emit the full multi-value list (~24 tokens) and scores 0.98. This is a distillation-induced output-completeness/format fix, **not** a long-context capability gain from quantization. (Consistent with the step500 checkpoints running slightly longer RULER answers overall — avg ~46 vs ~40 tokens.)

**Takeaway:** the headline "w4a4 > bf16 on RULER" is real in the numbers but misleading in interpretation. It is one subtask, driven by a training-induced formatting/completeness difference between two different models, not by 4-bit quantization improving the model.
